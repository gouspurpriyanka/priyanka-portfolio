AI EVOLUTION

An Interactive Timeline

Seventy-five years of artificial intelligence didn't move in a straight line — it boomed, froze, and boomed again. Scroll through the story, or jump straight to an era below.

Two winters, one pattern: hype outran reality, then the funding froze.

↓
1950

The Turing Test

Alan Turing asks whether a machine can be indistinguishable from a human. Frank Rosenblatt builds the Perceptron (1957). Arthur Samuel coins "Machine Learning" (1959).

Turing's 1950 paper, "Computing Machinery and Intelligence," proposed the test as a practical stand-in for the harder question "can machines think?" The term "Artificial Intelligence" itself wasn't coined until the 1956 Dartmouth Workshop.
1960s

Early Progress

AI grows through expert systems, games, translation, and government funding. Optimism peaks as researchers predict human-level AI within a generation.

ELIZA (1966), an early chatbot built to simulate a therapist, convinced some users they were talking to a real person — an early preview of debates about AI and trust that are still very much alive today.
1970s

First AI Winter

Slow computers, limited data, and high expectations lead to funding cuts. The Lighthill Report dampens enthusiasm in the UK and beyond.

Funding from DARPA and UK research councils dried up almost overnight after the 1973 Lighthill Report concluded AI had failed to deliver on its "grandiose objectives."
1980s

Expert Systems & 2nd Winter

XCON and Japan's Fifth Generation Project spark renewed funding. But expert systems become expensive and rigid; a second AI winter follows (1987–1993).

XCON was estimated to save its manufacturer millions of dollars a year by catching configuration errors — proof AI could pay for itself. But brittle, expensive expert systems couldn't scale, and by 1987 the market for specialized AI hardware collapsed almost overnight.
1990s

Recovery & Big Data

Machine learning, SVMs, Deep Blue, and the World Wide Web drive progress. Cloud computing and GPUs accelerate AI through the 2000s.

IBM's Deep Blue beat reigning world chess champion Garry Kasparov in 1997 — the first time a computer had beaten a sitting champion under standard tournament conditions.
2012

Deep Learning Boom

AlexNet wins ImageNet, launching modern deep learning. GANs, DeepFace, AlphaGo, the Transformer, BERT, and GPT-1 follow (2013–2018).

AlexNet roughly halved the ImageNet error rate compared to the runner-up, convincing the field almost overnight that deep neural networks — not hand-engineered features — were the way forward. "Attention Is All You Need," the paper that introduced the Transformer architecture underlying nearly every major AI model today, followed in 2017.
2020s

AI Goes Mainstream

GPT-3, AlphaFold2, GitHub Copilot, and ChatGPT bring AI to the mainstream. GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, Sora, and the EU AI Act reshape the landscape.

ChatGPT is widely reported to have reached an estimated 100 million users within about two months of its November 2022 launch — at the time, the fastest consumer-app adoption on record.
2025

Agentic AI Era

DeepSeek R1 and agentic AI go mainstream. Claude, GPT-5.x, and Gemini 3 push toward autonomous reasoning and action.

The shift is from "AI that answers" to "AI that acts": models increasingly plan multi-step tasks, use tools, and operate semi-autonomously across coding, research, and business workflows.

About This Artifact

Introduction & Description
This is an interactive timeline tracing the evolution of artificial intelligence from Alan Turing's 1950 test through today's agentic AI systems. It began as a static, hand-drawn-style graphic and was rebuilt here as a scroll-based, clickable web page so a visitor can explore the history at their own pace rather than just read it top to bottom.
Objective
To turn a piece of coursework — researching and mapping AI's history — into a shareable, audience-facing artifact that shows I can take technical content and present it clearly to people with a wide range of backgrounds, not just other AI/ML students.
Process
I started with the timeline content and milestones I developed for Workshop 1, then designed an interactive layer on top: a scroll-triggered progress spine, expandable detail cards for each milestone, quick-jump navigation by era, and a toggle that highlights the two AI "winters" to make the boom-and-bust pattern in AI history visible at a glance.
Tools & Technologies Used
  • HTML, CSS, and vanilla JavaScript (no frameworks required)
  • Google Fonts (Permanent Marker, Kalam, Inter)
  • GitHub Pages for hosting
Value Proposition
Unique Value: Shows I think about audience experience, not just content — the "Reveal the Winters" feature turns a fact I could have just stated in a sentence ("AI has had two funding winters") into something a visitor discovers and remembers.
Relevance: AI/ML is a field defined by hype cycles. Understanding how the field has boomed and stalled before — and why — is directly relevant to making good judgment calls about where it's headed next.
